john kane

john kane taught me everything i know about typography. other profs joked about him having an indesign tattoo on his arm, but when he saw mine he just said he didn't get it, asked why i didn't just print a picture and keep it in my pocket. one thing he did get was type, and he wasn't lying when he said he 'wrote the book on typography.'

type 2

perhaps the single most difficult project of my time as a designer, 'type 2' was a semester dedicated to designing our very own typeface. countless hours fine-tuning angles, contrasts, and symmetry, and i absolutely loved every minute of it. i won't forget the day john told me my work looked like a real typeface, and to this day i'm not sure anything has been more rewarding than the day i was able to type with it.

inspiration

drawing from classic serifs like baskerville and caslon, slabs like clarendon, egyptienne, and rockwell, and the unique angles of meta, i was interested in creating a hybrid serif of sorts, exploring forms with low contrast, elegant curves, and unconventional angles.

sketch

before we could even begin sketching our own letterforms, john required us to spend one full studio with a fountain pen exploring letters of all shapes and sizes. this undoubtedly helped us truly understand the foundation of typography and the derivitive of typefaces as we know them. from there, we could put pencil to paper and begin to craft our own.

after many hours of sketching, i'd finally settled on a semi-slab-serif style type

capitals

O isn't the most interesting form, but it's an essential one. E let me start to establish a serif style and experiment with angles that would appear in later letters. C came naturally as a combination of the two, and G followed suit. the remainder fell into place as i learned capitals do.

lowercase

lowercase letters weren't as straightforward as their capital counterparts, but i found them more interesting to work with and felt they brought out the uniqueness and personality i was looking to establish with sichel.

numbers

staying true to the traditional typographic roots through which we were taught, i opted to design both linine and oldstyle figures, or uppercase and lowercase numbers respectively.

symbols

i was through designing the ? symbol that i came to realize the name for my typeface—sichel. i'm disappointed to say the symbols were rushed and i never quite got them where i wanted to. i've told myself many times i'd revisit sichel someday, but sadly i've yet to. i'm still holding out hope for that, but in the meantime you can download sichel by clicking the broadside below and use it as you see fit.
